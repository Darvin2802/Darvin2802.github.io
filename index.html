The Problem

Mapping an IPP's renewable asset portfolio is foundational M&A due diligence. The team searched 6+ regulatory databases manually — 3–4 hours per company, no standard for complete. Citation gaps were silently undermining client trust. The real problem: nobody had defined "done."

Key Product Decisions
Decision 1
7 specialist roles, not one monolithic prompt
When outputs are wrong, roles make it clear which step failed. Auditability over convenience.
Decision 2
Mandatory source hierarchy: Company → SEC → Regulator → Media
Primary sources carry more evidentiary weight in M&A. Encoding this means every output reflects the same evidence standard.
Decision 3
Two research rounds before marking any field incomplete
A single pass misses assets in secondary sources. Two rounds reduces false "not found" flags.
Decision 4
Every output cell hyperlinked to its primary source
Clients don't just want data — they need to verify it. Auditability was the actual product.
Tradeoffs
Speed vs completeness: Two research rounds add time. A complete output clients can trust is more valuable than a faster incomplete one.
US-only vs US and Canada: Adding Canadian provincial regulators increased complexity — included because the deal universe required it.
Outcome

PM Lesson
Define "done" before you define "fast." The team's problem looked like a speed problem. It was a standards problem. Faster inconsistency is still inconsistency.

The Problem

Ctrl+F keyword search was used to extract M&A transactions. At 1–2 hours per document it was slow — but the real problem: keyword search has a hard architectural limit. M&A language is deliberately varied. Footnote and financing-section deals were being missed systematically, not randomly.

Key Product Decisions
Decision 1
Two distinct passes, not one comprehensive list
Larger primary list increases noise. Different pass types target different language patterns — more precise than more comprehensive.
Decision 2
Mandatory cross-check routine after both passes
Building explicit doubt about completeness — targeting footnotes regardless of pass results — closes the gap.
Decision 3
Historical transactions captured, not just current
M&A filings reference completed, pending, and historical transactions. Excluding historical ones loses valuation context.
Decision 4
Structured narrative output, not a raw data table
Analysts need context, not just fields. Output is client-ready — no reformatting required.
Tradeoffs
Precision vs recall: Pass 2 flags shown separately so analysts know which hits require more scrutiny — transparency over automation of judgment.
Automation vs analyst judgment: The workflow extracts and contextualises but doesn't make significance judgments. That stays with the human.

When a team has a coverage problem, check the tool before blaming the process. The gap was that nobody questioned whether keyword search was the right tool for contextual document understanding.

The Problem

A junior analyst spent 2–3 hours every morning scanning press release platforms. The obvious fix was automation. But the real problem was structural: the entire team's daily deal intelligence depended on one person. If they were sick, coverage dropped and nobody knew. This wasn't an inefficient process — it was a fragile one.

Key Product Decisions
Decision 1
Framed as resilience problem, not efficiency problem
A faster fragile process is still fragile. The goal was to eliminate the single point of failure entirely.
Decision 2
Explicit exclusion list, not just inclusion logic
Defining what NOT to include — operational milestones, earnings — was as important as inclusions. Without exclusions, noise creeps in daily.
Decision 3
MVP matched existing process exactly
Adoption requires trust, trust requires familiarity. v1 matched the analyst's format — team validated against what they knew.
Decision 4
Three-section output with full coverage transparency
Showing exactly what was scanned made the output verifiable. Teams don't trust a black box.
Tradeoffs
Fully automated vs analyst-triggered: v1 kept human-triggered to preserve a quality check during trust-building. Full automation is a v2 decision.
Speed vs transparency: Coverage summary takes slightly longer but built trust faster than speed would have.
Outcome

Expert System · Deal Intelligence · GitHub Open Source
M&A Deal Memo Intelligence System
Turned 2–4 hours of variable-quality analyst work into a 60-minute system encoding senior analyst judgment — so a junior analyst produces a better-sourced memo than a senior analyst working manually.
60%
Time saved

The Problem

Every M&A transaction required a sourced deal memo. Analysts wrote these from scratch — 2–4 hours per deal — with quality varying by seniority. In M&A intelligence, an unsourced fact is a credibility problem that damages client trust. Quality problem disguised as a speed problem.

Key Product Decisions
Decision 1
Accuracy as primary constraint, not speed
In M&A intelligence, a wrong fact is worse than a slow fact. Speed was a byproduct. Quality floor was the requirement.
Decision 2
10-step mandatory sequence, not a single prompt
Sequence mirrors how a careful analyst works. Separating verification, retrieval, and mapping prevents steps from compressing each other.
Decision 3
15 hard anti-hallucination rules, not guidelines
Rules stop the system and request clarification. "Likely" and "appears to be" prohibited unless quoted from source.
Decision 4
±7 day presentation search window
Investor presentations published within a week of announcements are consistently missed manually. Encoding this as mandatory catches documents that change memo quality.
Tradeoffs
Speed vs accuracy: 10-step sequence is slower than one prompt. Accepted deliberately — a wrong memo is worse than a delayed one.
Internal tool vs public system: Publishing on GitHub exposed methodology but created external validation and a forcing function to document properly.
Outcome

PM Lesson
The best AI product decisions encode expert judgment, not just expert speed. Speed was a byproduct. Encoding judgment is the product.
